> **The problem first.** Bob taps "send" in his banking app. That tap has to become something his bank's payment engine can validate, store, and execute without a human ever reading it â€” and it has to carry enough identity that, forty seconds and three banks later, Sweety still sees *"Invoice 0042"* on her statement. What does that tap actually transmit, field by field, and how is it different from what his bank sends onward?

The **pain family deep dive** covered *why* pain exists and its three-level shape. This is the **field-by-field** companion to its headline message: **pain.001**, the Customer Credit Transfer Initiation â€” the request every push payment is born from.

The one line to hold onto: **pain.001 is you instructing your bank; no money moves inside it.** It is a request. The money moves later, in the pacs.008 your bank builds from it.

## One message, one *or four hundred* payments

A pain.001 is not necessarily one payment. It is a **file** with three nested levels, and that nesting is what makes it scale from a single tap to a whole payroll run:

- **Group Header (`GrpHdr`)** â€” file-wide facts, stated **once**: a message id, a timestamp, the total number of transactions, a control sum.
- **Payment Information (`PmtInf`)** â€” one block per shared set of parameters: the debtor (Bob), his account, the debit date, the payment method. A file may hold many `PmtInf` blocks.
- **Credit Transfer Transaction (`CdtTrfTxInf`)** â€” one block **per payee**: Sweety, her account, the amount, the reference.

Bob's app sends one `GrpHdr`, one `PmtInf`, one `CdtTrfTxInf`. A company's payroll sends one `GrpHdr`, one `PmtInf`, and four hundred `CdtTrfTxInf` blocks â€” shared facts once, one transaction per employee. Same message, same shape.

## The four identifiers everyone confuses

More payments stall over muddled ids than almost anything else. A pain.001 carries four, at different levels, doing different jobs â€” plus the UETR that rides alongside:

- **`MsgId`** (in `GrpHdr`) â€” identifies the **file/message** itself. Scope: this transmission. The bank uses it to detect a duplicate *file*.
- **`PmtInfId`** (in `PmtInf`) â€” identifies the **batch** (this debit run). Scope: the payment-information block.
- **`InstrId`** (in `CdtTrfTxInf`) â€” the **instruction** id, meaningful between *you and your bank* only. It is **not** guaranteed to travel onward.
- **`EndToEndId`** (in `CdtTrfTxInf`) â€” **your** reference for the payment, and the one that **must survive every hop** untouched, all the way to Sweety. This is how "Invoice 0042" reaches her statement.
- **`UETR`** â€” a globally unique UUIDv4 that tracks the payment across every bank end to end. Your bank stamps it (if the app didn't) and it stays fixed for the payment's whole life.

Rule of thumb: **`InstrId` is local and disposable; `EndToEndId` and `UETR` are sacred and end-to-end.**

## A sample pain.001, annotated

```xml
<CstmrCdtTrfInitn>
  <GrpHdr>
    <MsgId>BOBAPP-20260701-0001</MsgId>          <!-- the file's id -->
    <CreDtTm>2026-07-01T08:15:00+04:00</CreDtTm>
    <NbOfTxs>1</NbOfTxs>                          <!-- must match the count below -->
    <CtrlSum>33000.00</CtrlSum>                   <!-- must equal the sum of amounts -->
    <InitgPty><Nm>Bob Marsh</Nm></InitgPty>
  </GrpHdr>
  <PmtInf>
    <PmtInfId>BOB-RUN-0042</PmtInfId>             <!-- the batch id -->
    <PmtMtd>TRF</PmtMtd>
    <ReqdExctnDt>2026-07-01</ReqdExctnDt>
    <Dbtr><Nm>Bob Marsh</Nm></Dbtr>
    <DbtrAcct><Id><IBAN>AE070331234567890123456</IBAN></Id></DbtrAcct>
    <DbtrAgt><FinInstnId><BICFI>EBILAEAD</BICFI></FinInstnId></DbtrAgt>
    <CdtTrfTxInf>
      <PmtId>
        <InstrId>BOB-INSTR-1</InstrId>            <!-- local, may not travel -->
        <EndToEndId>BOB-INV0042</EndToEndId>      <!-- travels untouched -->
        <UETR>eb6305c9-1f7c-4a9b-9b1e-2c2f4e7a91d4</UETR>
      </PmtId>
      <Amt><InstdAmt Ccy="INR">33000.00</InstdAmt></Amt>
      <Cdtr><Nm>Sweety Rao</Nm></Cdtr>
      <CdtrAcct><Id><IBAN>IN80HDFC0001234567890123</IBAN></Id></CdtrAcct>
      <CdtrAgt><FinInstnId><BICFI>HDFCINBB</BICFI></FinInstnId></CdtrAgt>
      <RmtInf><Ustrd>Invoice 0042 â€” June freelance</Ustrd></RmtInf>
    </CdtTrfTxInf>
  </PmtInf>
</CstmrCdtTrfInitn>
```

The cast is the one from Level 100: Bob is the **Debtor**, Sweety the **Creditor**. Two validations bite here before anything else: `NbOfTxs` must equal the number of `CdtTrfTxInf` blocks, and `CtrlSum` must equal the sum of their amounts. Get either wrong and the whole file is rejected â€” the message is self-checking by design.

## How the bank answers: pain.002

Bob's bank doesn't reply with money â€” it replies with a **pain.002** Customer Payment Status Report:

- **Accepted** (`ACCP` / `ACSP`) â€” the instruction is valid and moving.
- **Rejected** (`RJCT`) â€” with a **reason code** (e.g. `AC01` incorrect account, `AM04` insufficient funds) pinpointing which transaction failed and why.
- **Pending** (`PDNG`) â€” accepted, awaiting something (a cut-off, a check).

This is the tick â€” or the error â€” Bob sees in his app. Still, no money has moved. The pain.002 is a receipt about the *request*, not the payment.

## The handoff: what your bank keeps, what it adds

For every accepted instruction, Bob's bank becomes the **debtor agent** and turns the pain.001 into a **pacs.008** â€” the bank-to-bank message that actually moves money. In that translation:

- **Preserved:** the `EndToEndId` (`BOB-INV0042`) and the `UETR` â€” untouched, so the payment stays traceable end to end.
- **Added:** interbank settlement fields â€” `IntrBkSttlmAmt`, `IntrBkSttlmDt`, settlement method, and the agent chain (instructing/instructed agents) that routes it toward HDFCINBB.
- **Dropped:** the purely local `InstrId` and the file-level `GrpHdr`/`CtrlSum` framing â€” those were between Bob and his bank.

That is the exact seam where the **pain** family ends and the **pacs** family begins.

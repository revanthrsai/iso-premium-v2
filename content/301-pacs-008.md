> **The problem first.** Bob's bank has accepted his instruction. The money is leaving Dubai for Bangalore. But the *next* bank in the chain has never seen Bob, never seen this payment, and won't take a phone call about it. How does Bob's bank tell it — precisely, unambiguously, in a way a machine can act on without a human reading it — *"pay Sweety ₹33,000, here's everyone involved, here's the reference"*?

It sends a **pacs.008**. This is the bank-to-bank message that carries a customer credit transfer between institutions. It is the ISO 20022 replacement for the old SWIFT **MT103**, and it is, by volume, one of the most important messages in global finance. If you only ever learn one message in depth, learn this one.

## What the name means

ISO 20022 message names look cryptic until you decode them once:

- **pacs** — *Payments Clearing and Settlement*. The family of bank-to-bank payment messages.
- **008** — the specific message in that family: the FI-to-FI customer credit transfer.

So `pacs.008` = *"the clearing-and-settlement family's message for moving a customer's credit transfer between financial institutions."* Its full technical id is `pacs.008.001.08` — the trailing numbers are the variant and version.

## Who sends it, and to whom

A `pacs.008` is **strictly bank-to-bank**. It is *not* the message Bob's app sends — that's a `pain.001` (a customer *instructing* their bank). Bob's bank receives his `pain.001`, then **turns it into a `pacs.008`** to pass the payment onward.

- **Created by:** the Debtor Agent — Bob's bank.
- **Received by:** the Creditor Agent — Sweety's bank — directly or through one or more intermediary agents.

Every hop preserves who pays whom. That's the whole point of the structure.

## The anatomy, block by block

A `pacs.008` has two big parts: a **Group Header** (facts about the file) and one or more **Credit Transfer Transaction** blocks (the actual payments).

### Group Header (`GrpHdr`) — the cover sheet

```xml
<GrpHdr>
  <MsgId>BNKAUS33-20260627-000400</MsgId>
  <CreDtTm>2026-06-27T09:30:00+04:00</CreDtTm>
  <NbOfTxs>1</NbOfTxs>
  <SttlmInf><SttlmMtd>INDA</SttlmMtd></SttlmInf>
</GrpHdr>
```

- **`MsgId`** — a reference for *this hop only*. It changes every time the message is forwarded. Point-to-point, disposable.
- **`CreDtTm`** — when the message was created, **with a UTC offset** (`+04:00`). Drop the offset and the receiver can't place the payment in time. This is a real rejection cause.
- **`NbOfTxs`** — how many transactions are inside.
- **`SttlmMtd`** — *how* the banks settle: through accounts they hold with each other (the nostro/vostro from Level 100) or via a clearing system.

### The transaction (`CdtTrfTxInf`) — who, how much, references

```xml
<CdtTrfTxInf>
  <PmtId>
    <EndToEndId>BOB-INV0042</EndToEndId>
    <UETR>eb6305c9-1f7c-4a9b-9b1e-2c2f4e7a91d4</UETR>
  </PmtId>
  <IntrBkSttlmAmt Ccy="USD">400.00</IntrBkSttlmAmt>
  <Dbtr><Nm>Bob Marsh</Nm></Dbtr>
  <DbtrAgt><FinInstnId><BICFI>EBILAEAD</BICFI></FinInstnId></DbtrAgt>
  <CdtrAgt><FinInstnId><BICFI>HDFCINBB</BICFI></FinInstnId></CdtrAgt>
  <Cdtr><Nm>Sweety Rao</Nm></Cdtr>
  <RmtInf><Ustrd>Invoice 0042 — June freelance</Ustrd></RmtInf>
</CdtTrfTxInf>
```

The cast you already know, now in tags:

- **`Dbtr` / `Cdtr`** — the Debtor (Bob, who pays) and Creditor (Sweety, who is paid).
- **`DbtrAgt` / `CdtrAgt`** — their banks, each identified by a **BIC** (Bank Identifier Code) — 8 or 11 characters.
- **`IntrBkSttlmAmt`** — the amount that settles *between the banks*, with its currency as an attribute.
- **`RmtInf` / `Ustrd`** — the remittance information: what the payment is *for*.

## The three identifiers everyone confuses

This is the single most common interview question on `pacs.008`, so commit it to memory:

- **`MsgId`** — point-to-point. Rotates on every hop. Means nothing end to end.
- **`EndToEndId`** — the **customer's** reference. Travels **unchanged** from the first bank to the last. Must survive every hop intact.
- **`UETR`** — the Unique End-to-end Transaction Reference. A globally unique **UUIDv4** that identifies the *whole* payment across *every* bank, used for tracking (this is what powers "where is my payment?").

## What breaks — the real rejections

These aren't hypothetical. They are the failure modes that get payments NAK'd in production:

- **Placeholder references.** An `EndToEndId` of `NOTPROVIDED`, `NA`, or empty gets rejected. It must be a real value, ≤35 characters, that survives every hop.
- **Missing timezone.** A `CreDtTm` with no UTC offset is ambiguous across borders and is bounced.
- **Malformed UETR.** Uppercase letters or the wrong version/variant bits and it's no longer a valid UUIDv4 — rejected.
- **Truncated names.** The old MT103 had a fixed 35-character name field; long names got clipped, and sanctions screening then flagged the *wrong* party. ISO 20022 keeps the whole name — but only if the upstream system actually sends it.
